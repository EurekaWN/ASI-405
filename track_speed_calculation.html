Tracked locomotion systems are widely used in military and agricultural platforms because of their excellent terrain adaptability and traction, and their ability to climb obstacles exceeding twice the roller diameter (Wong, 2001; Liang et al., 2023). However, their higher internal resistance and lower speed generally make them less efficient than wheeled systems (Wong & Huang, 2006; Chołodowski & Dudziński, 2021). Since translational speed is critical in the RoboMaster competition, a preliminary power–speed analysis is carried out to evaluate whether a tracked configuration is feasible.

Steady-state power balance

The robot is designed with a mass of 20 kg and an available mechanical power of 85 W. A steady-state conservation of energy approach is used:

Motor output = Kinetic power + Losses
Pmotor = ½ m v² + (Froll + Fint + …) v
P = τ · ω

Resistive forces:

Froll = Cr m g
Fint = Cint m g
  • Rolling resistance: Cr = 0.03
    Pneumatic wheels ≈ 0.01 (Łukasz, 2018).
  • Internal motion resistance: Cint = 0.12 (design value)
    Includes bending losses, tension losses, bearing losses.
Scaling of internal losses
  1. Reference: 2-ton tracked prototype Cint,h ≈ 0.09 (SpringerLink)
  2. Bending-loss similitude:
    Cbend ∝ (E B t³) / (R² m g)
    Downscaling factor s = (mr/mh)1/3 = (20/2000)1/3 ≈ 0.215 Pure scaling ⇒ multiplier ≈ 1/s ≈ 4.65 (conservative upper bound).
  3. Empirical data for smaller platforms:
    • 720 kg tracked platform: C ≈ 0.14–0.28
    • 1.7 t rubber-belt prototype: C ≈ 0.15–0.21
    • Small robots on hard floors: C > 0.20 (MDPI)
    Shows losses increase as size decreases.
Final coefficients used
  • Internal resistance: Cint = 0.12
    Valid range: 0.10–0.18 (supported by SpringerLink + scaling)
  • Rolling/interface resistance: Croll = 0.06
    Range: 0.04–0.12 (MDPI small-robot data)

These values are substituted into the steady-state power equation to estimate the achievable flat-ground speed and evaluate whether a tracked design can satisfy the RoboMaster speed requirements.
