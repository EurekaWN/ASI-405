4. Design Thinking Process

This section documents the overall design thinking process used to arrive at the final bipedal wheel-leg robot concept. Rather than jumping directly into a single solution, multiple design directions were explored, evaluated, and iteratively refined using design specifications, literature review, morphological charts, and quantitative analysis of key locomotion concepts.

4.1 Design Specification (Bipedal Wheel Leg Robot)

The design specifications for the bipedal wheel-leg robot are derived directly from the RoboMaster competition rules and the strategic needs of the NUS Calibur team. These specifications act as hard design constraints and ensure that every concept considered remains both competition legal and practically deployable.

Click to expand: Key design specifications
  • Robot type: Standard robot chassis (primary combat platform).
  • Compliant with all current RMUC mechanical, electrical, and safety rules.
  • Maximum dimensions: within 600 × 600 × 500 mm initial, expandable to 800 × 800 × 800 mm.
  • Total mass target: approximately 20–25 kg (excluding referee system).
  • Power system: ≤ 300 Wh pack energy, ≤ 30 V bus voltage, ≤ 2200 J supercapacitor energy.
  • Mobility target: high-speed movement on flat terrain with efficient 200 mm step-climbing capability.
  • Compatibility with existing gimbal, referee system, armour modules, and sensors used by the Calibur team.

4.2 Literature Review of the Past Designs

This subsection reviews relevant climbing and all-terrain robot designs from both research literature and existing RoboMaster robots. The goal is to understand the strengths and limitations of different locomotion strategies—continuous tracks, articulated legs, wheel-leg hybrids, and transformable wheels—and how they influence step-climbing and terrain adaptability.

Click to expand: Summary of reviewed design families
  • Tracked robots – excellent terrain contact and stability, but higher mechanical complexity, higher internal losses, and potential speed penalties.
  • Legged robots – high adaptability and obstacle negotiation, but require sophisticated control and often sacrifice top speed and robustness.
  • Wheel-leg hybrids – combine rolling efficiency with active step-climbing through leg motion, offering a promising balance for RoboMaster applications.
  • Planetary and transformable wheels – mechanically clever and capable of large effective radii, but often complex to design and maintain.

Insights from these designs directly inform which concepts are shortlisted for further analysis in the morphological chart and subsequent design sense-making steps.

4.3 Morphological Chart

To systematically explore the design space, a morphological chart is used to decompose the robot into key functional blocks (locomotion type, step strategy, suspension concept, wheel geometry, etc.) and to generate multiple concept combinations. Each concept is scored against weighted performance criteria such as flat-ground speed, step-climbing capability, stability, power consumption, and mechanical complexity.

The interactive morphological gallery embedded in this report allows each concept to be visualised and compared dynamically. Hovering over a design reveals its parameter scores and a short description, making the trade-offs between concepts more intuitive.

4.4 Design Sense Making – Continuous Track

Continuous tracks are a natural candidate for all-terrain RoboMaster robots because of their large contact area and ability to maintain traction over uneven ground. In this project, a tracked configuration was analysed in detail to evaluate whether it could meet the required 200 mm step performance while staying within speed, power, and weight constraints.

The analysis included rolling resistance, internal track losses, and available motor torque, which together determine the achievable robot speed and climbing capability.

4.4.1 Continuous Track Speed Calculation

Click to expand: Track speed and power calculations

This subsection documents the estimation of the track-driven robot speed based on motor characteristics, gear ratios, and rolling resistance coefficients. The key steps include:

  • Estimating rolling resistance (Croll) and internal losses in the undercarriage.
  • Computing maximum tractive effort from motor torque and sprocket radius.
  • Relating available thrust to steady-state speed on flat ground.
  • Checking whether the resulting speed and acceleration meet competition needs.

The results show that, although a tracked design offers strong terrain capability, the penalty in internal losses and mechanical complexity makes it less attractive compared to lighter wheel-leg and linkage-based solutions for this specific RoboMaster task.

4.5 Wheel Leg Holonomic Analysis

After evaluating the continuous track design, attention is shifted to wheel-leg locomotion. This subsection analyses the kinematics and mobility characteristics of wheel-leg concepts, including whether they behave as holonomic or non-holonomic systems and how that affects battlefield manoeuvrability.

Click to expand: Overview of wheel-leg mobility analysis

The analysis examines:

  • Feasible motion directions when the legs are in ground-contact phase.
  • Turning radius and pivoting behaviour during step-climbing.
  • Impact of leg geometry on stability and gimbal aiming performance.

These insights help filter out purely holonomic but mechanically fragile designs in favour of robust non-holonomic variants that still provide adequate manoeuvrability for RoboMaster gameplay.

4.6 Linkage Design Analysis (Bipedal with Linkage)

The bipedal concept with linkages is then studied as a candidate that can amplify torque and enable controlled step-climbing without overloading the actuators. The linkage geometry is tuned to provide favourable mechanical advantage near the step while maintaining a compact configuration during normal driving.

Click to expand: Linkage configuration and torque amplification

The analysis focuses on:

  • Mapping motor torque to ground reaction forces through the linkage.
  • Identifying singular configurations and unstable regions.
  • Ensuring that the robot can support its own weight when quasi-static on the step edge.

These results justify using a linkage-based bipedal architecture over simpler wheel-only designs for reliable 200 mm step-climbing.

4.7 Big Wheel Design Analysis

A big-wheel concept is also evaluated, where the robot attempts to clear the step primarily through a large effective wheel radius and controlled “hopping” or rolling motion onto the riser. This approach is attractive because it can simplify the mechanism, but it places strict requirements on wheel size, centre-of-mass position, and available motor power.

Click to expand: Step-climbing and hopping feasibility

The study includes:

  • Geometric conditions for the wheel to contact and roll over a 200 mm step.
  • Required vertical and horizontal velocities for smooth “hop” motions.
  • Checking whether the launch speed is achievable within motor torque and power limits.

The analysis shows that while large wheels can theoretically climb the step, the associated compromises in packaging, weight distribution, and game layout make this option less favourable than the final bipedal wheel-leg linkage design.

4.8 Design Analysis Summary

The analyses in Sections 4.4–4.7 are summarised by comparing each concept against the weighted design criteria. Continuous tracks, big wheels, and alternative wheel-leg configurations are systematically eliminated in favour of a bipedal wheel-leg design with linkage-driven step-climbing.

Click to expand: Shortlisted concept rationale
  • Continuous track: strong terrain capability, but higher loss, complexity, and packaging challenges.
  • Pure big wheel: geometrically feasible but bulky and less compatible with RoboMaster chassis constraints.
  • Bipedal wheel-leg with linkage: good compromise between step-climbing performance, agility, and structural robustness.

This leads to the selection of the bipedal wheel-leg robot as the main candidate for detailed design and prototyping in the next section.

4.9 Initial Design Concept

Based on the design thinking process and comparative analyses, an initial bipedal wheel-leg concept is proposed. The robot uses a pair of actuated wheel-legs that can perform both high-speed rolling on flat terrain and controlled stepping motions over 200 mm obstacles.

This initial concept provides the foundation for the detailed CAD design, structural validation, and prototyping described in Section 5: Finalised Main Design Concept Validation and Prototyping.
