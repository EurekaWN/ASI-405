2. Competition Background Information

2.1 Robomaster Competition Specification and Robot Requirements

For the Standard robot, which is the main strategic unit in a team’s lineup, it serves as the primary combat and mobility platform. It is responsible for mid-range combat, rapid map control, and tactical flexibility, making it the main focus of this project. The objective of this work is to redesign the Standard chassis to include step-climbing capability while maintaining compliance with RoboMaster competition rules.

Figure 1: Explanation put here.

The arena features multi-level platforms divided by trenches, slopes, and most importantly the central 200 mm vertical step leading to the highland (Refer to Figure 1)—the most strategically valuable region. Although current RMUC rules do not require step-climbing capability, terrain traversal is a major game-changer. Achieving reliable 200 mm step-climbing provides significant tactical advantage while remaining within all mechanical, electrical, and firmware regulations.

2.2 Robomaster Strategical Considerations

Figure 2: Explanation put here.

The RoboMaster battlefield rewards positional control, coordinated firepower, and efficient resource usage. Scaling the 200 mm central step (Route 1, refer to Figure 2) unlocks the Terrain Cross Buff, which gives:

  • +25% defence bonus
  • Up to 5× faster shooting cooldown
  • Immediate access to high-value zones

Standard robots normally access the island using long ramps (Route 3), which delays arrival and increases exposure to enemy fire. A step-climbing platform enables Route 1 access— directly scaling the 200 mm obstacle.

This provides:

  • Speed Advantage: Shortest path to high-value positions
  • Strategic Redundancy: Alternative routes if ramps are blocked
  • Psychological Impact: Unexpected mobility disrupts enemy planning
  • Autonomous Path Planning: Unlocks AI-driven navigation options

Therefore, integrating step-climbing ability directly supports the team’s strategy of aggressive mid-map control and high-ground dominance.

2.3 Prediction of Future Rules

This project considers not only current strategic requirements but also the evolving trend of RoboMaster rules. Anticipating these developments provides a forward-looking advantage in adaptability and competitiveness.

2.3.1 Robomaster Competition Map Evolving Trend

Figure 3: Explanation put here.

Recent RoboMaster competitions show a clear shift toward more mobility-demanding terrain:

  • 2024: Mild bumpy road → focus on suspension & vibration control.
  • 2025: Major changes: 200 mm and 100 mm steps, “Terrain Cross” buff.
  • 2026 (released Oct 2025): Introduced 40 mm-deep wave terrain, eliminating flat paths entirely.

These updates highlight the growing emphasis on terrain adaptability, reinforcing the need for multi-terrain robot platforms in future competitions.

2.3.2 Global Trend of All-Terrain Robots (click to expand)

Figure 4: Explanation put here.

All-terrain robotics has evolved significantly since early research platforms such as RHex (1998). Commercial adoption accelerated after 2010 with applications in defence, mining, inspection, and agriculture. Global demand increased rapidly from 2020 onwards, with market studies projecting strong growth into the 2030s.

The development of this step-climbing RoboMaster Standard robot aligns with these trends, combining competition needs, global robotics movement, and evolving RMUC terrain design. The result is a forward-leaning platform that remains adaptable to future rule changes and new terrain challenges.

2.4 Project Stakeholders (click to expand)

This project involves 2 main stakeholders whose needs and expectations shape the design direction and performance requirements of the robot platform:

Figure 5: Explanation put here.

  • NUS Calibur RoboMaster Team – primary users of the robot. They require a robust, competition-ready chassis that can be integrated with existing gimbal, referee, and control systems while remaining maintainable between matches.
  • Competition Organisers and Rule Setters – indirectly influence the design by defining mechanical constraints, safety requirements, and map terrain. The robot must comply fully with these regulations while remaining adaptable to future changes.

Understanding these stakeholders clarifies why the project emphasises reliability, maintainability, documentation quality, and forward compatibility alongside pure performance and terrain capability.
