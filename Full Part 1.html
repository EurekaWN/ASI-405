1. Introduction


RoboMaster is an international robotics competition founded by DJI that blends engineering innovation with team-based tactical combat. Two teams battle using custom-built robots —such as infantry, hero, engineer, and aerial units —that shoot projectiles and register hits through onboard armor sensors. Victory depends on mobility, positioning, resource control, and ultimately destroying the opponent ’s base.

This project focuses on developing a RoboMaster-compliant robot chassis capable of reliably climbing a 200 mm step and traversing complex terrain. Such capability provides strategic advantages by enabling faster access to elevated zones, bypassing chokepoints, and securing superior firing positions. The design prioritizes terrain performance without compromising core metrics such as stability, agility, or shooting accuracy, while adhering to current competition rules and anticipating future updates.

The work encompasses the full development cycle of a RoboMaster combat robot, including mechanical design, prototyping, structural validation, fabrication, electrical and firmware integration, and ultimately kinematic and dynamic motion control.

2.2 Robomaster Strategical Considerations

The RoboMaster battlefield rewards positional control, coordinated firepower, and efficient resource usage. Crossing over to the  central island using the 200mm Steps provides critical buffs, including +25 % defence and reduced cooldown to as much as 5 times for shooting, which can determine the outcome of a confrontation.

Standard robots are responsible for early-game map control and mid-range combat. However, current chassis designs restrict them to only using long ramps (Route 3) to access the central platform (no buff), delaying their arrival and exposing them to enemy fire.

Thus, integrating step-climbing ability is essential as it  directly supports the team ’s strategy of aggressive mid-map control and high-ground dominance.

4.4.1 Continuous Track Speed Calculation

Tracked locomotion systems are highly regarded for their superior terrain adaptability and traction, making them a common choice for military tanks and agricultural machinery due to their ability to traverse rough terrain and climb obstacles exceeding twice the roller diameter (Wong, 2001; Liang et al., 2023). However, their design inherently leads to higher internal resistance and lower speed, resulting in reduced efficiency compared to wheeled systems (Wong &Huang, 2006; Cho łodowski &Dudzi ński, 2021). Given the speed constraints critical in the Robot Master Competition, a preliminary power –speed analysis was conducted to evaluate the feasibility of employing a tracked configuration.

Explanation of Calculation

In designing the robot with a mass of 20  kg and an available mechanical power of 85  W, the steady ‐state speed on flat ground is estimated by balancing drive power and resistive losses. Resistive losses are divided into two main components: interface (rolling) losses and internal under ‑carriage motion losses.


Due to the difficulty in locating the direction of the internal friction force and rolling resistance force. Steady state conservation of energy equation is used.

Steady State - Conservation of energy equation
  Motor output power =  Kinetic energy of linear motion + Power losses
Motor input x Motor efficiency = ½ m v^2 + ( Rolling resistance + Track internal friction + Slip + …) * Velocity
Power Input = Torque x Angular Velocity  ( τ* ω)

Frictional Force Losses are in terms of Coefficient of Resistance * Weight of the Robot

In this case:

Coefficient of Rolling Resistance Cr = 0.03.
The rolling resistance of tracked systems is typically estimated to be more than twice that of pneumatic tyres. Pneumatic wheels, such as those used in wheelchairs, exhibit rolling resistance values that depend on tyre pressure and the type of surface. According to M. Sydor (2018), the rolling resistance coefficient at nominal pressure on a hard surface is approximately 0.01 (Łukasz, 2018).

Coefficient of Internal Resistance Ci = 0.2

Internal motion resistance on hard ground is dominated by:

  • track bending losses around rollers/idlers,
  • tension/guide and bearing losses,
  • an optional friction-amplification due to the track –wheel interface coefficient

C_int = C_bend + C_tens + C_bear ,   F_int = C_int ×m ×g

2) Bending-loss similitude (dimensional scaling)

For a rubber belt, bending “effort ”per unit distance scales with flexural rigidity EI and curvature 1/R. A consistent force-like scale is:

F_bend ∝EI / R ²  ⇒  C_bend ∝EI / (R ²×m ×g)

For a belt, the second moment I ≈(1/12) ×B ×t ³(width B, thickness t), so EI ≈k_E ×E ×B ×t ³where E is the effective elastic modulus and k_E is an order-1 geometric factor.

Scaling from a heavy reference (subscript h) to the robot (subscript r):

C_bend,r ≈C_bend,h ×(E_r/E_h) ×(t_r/t_h)³×(B_r/B_h) ×(R_h/R_r)²×(m_h/m_r)

If constructions are similar, take E_r ≈E_h.

3) Tension/guide losses scaling

Let S be track tension per width (N/m). With F_tens ∝S:

C_tens ∝S / (m ×g)  ⇒ C_tens,r ≈C_tens,h ×[S_r / (m_r ×g)] / [S_h / (m_h ×g)]

4) Bearing/roller losses scaling (optional)

With n_r rollers, bearing friction factor f_b, track tension T, and bearing radius r_b:

F_bear ∝n_r ×f_b ×(T / r_b)

⇒ C_bear,r ≈C_bear,h ×(n_r,r / n_r,h) ×(f_b,r / f_b,h) ×(T_r / r_b,r) / (T_h / r_b,h) ×(m_h / m_r)

If details are unavailable, fold bearing losses into C_tens.

5) Friction amplification from the figure/table

If the source gives a percentage increase δξ(μ, R_bend/R_wheel) in bending resistance versus μand geometry, apply it as a multiplier:

C_bend,r(μ) = C_bend,r ×[1 + δξ(μ, R_bend / R_wheel)]

6) Combined estimate

C_int,r ≈C_bend,h ×(t_r/t_h)³×(B_r/B_h) ×(R_h/R_r)²×(m_h/m_r) ×(1 + δξ) + C_tens,h ×[S_r / (m_r ×g)] / [S_h / (m_h ×g)] + C_bear,h (optional)

1) Pick a reference from measured data

Use the 2.0 t rubber-track prototype as a clean baseline with measured C_int,h ≈0.09 (midpoint of 0.08 –0.10). [Source: SpringerLink]

2) Scale by bending similitude (order-of-magnitude)

For geometrically similar belts, bending-loss similitude gives (see derivation):

C_bend ∝(E ×B ×t ³) / (R ²×m ×g)

If we assume similar construction (E, B, t, R) scale with the vehicle ’s characteristic length L and mass scales as m ∝L ³, then:

C_bend ∝L ⁴/ (L ²×L ³) = 1/L ⇒when down-scaling by s = L_r / L_h,  C_bend,r ≈C_bend,h ×(1/s).

Take s ≈(m_r / m_h)^(1/3) = (20 / 2000)^(1/3) ≈0.215.

Pure similitude factor ≈1/s ≈4.65.

That would push a bending-dominated coefficient from 0.09 →~0.42. This is a conservative upper bound; in practice, softer elastomers and different track constructions reduce E and effective t, and some losses do not scale that harshly. Cho łodowski &Dudzi ński also show tension/bearing losses contribute and wheel-diameter (bending radius) matters. [Source: SpringerLink]

3) Temper with empirical small-robot evidence

Measured values for lighter systems in the same Springer table already show 0.14 –0.28 for a 720 kg articulated platform and 0.15 –0.21 for a 1.7 t rubber-belt prototype —well above heavy tanks (~0.025 –0.065). Meanwhile, small-robot studies report overall rolling loss on hard surfaces can exceed 0.20 (that ’s the external rolling part, not internal), so picking an internal coefficient in the 0.10 –0.18 band for a 20 kg tracked robot on smooth floors is defensible and avoids the over-aggressive 4.65 ×scaling. 4.5 Wheel Leg Holonomic Analysis.

After eliminating the tracked design, we focused on analyzing the wheel-leg, linkage-leg, and big-wheel concepts, each of which demonstrates its own distinct functions and characteristics.

The wheel-leg design introduces an additional degree of freedom, allowing vertical movement. This enables the robot to “jump ”or lift itself over obstacles. It also functions as an enhanced suspension system —the leg actuated by motor torque can absorb or compensate for vibrations when moving over uneven terrain.

However, the main limitation of the wheel-leg design is that it is non-holonomic. Compared to the mecanum or omni-wheel configurations currently used by the team, non-holonomic systems lack the ability to move freely in any direction. In RoboMaster competitions, holonomic mobility offers a significant advantage during confrontations —it allows the robot to quickly strafe in multiple directions while maintaining the so-called “Beyblade mode ”, where the chassis spins rapidly to evade projectiles.

In contrast, a non-holonomic robot can only rotate on the spot and cannot perform agile lateral maneuvers, limiting its responsiveness in fast-paced combat situations. Nonetheless, because the wheel-leg design offers improved terrain adaptability, its motion performance is simulated to determine whether this trade-off is acceptable for the intended application.

The motion simulation in MATLAB adopts real physical constraints, such as the non-holonomic kinematic equation of a differential-drive robot:

which governs how the robot ’s position (x,y)(x, y)(x,y) and orientation θ\theta θevolve based on its forward velocity vvv and angular velocity ω\omega ω.

In the code, this constraint is reflected in the following section:

vx2 = v2 * cos(pose_2w(3));

vy2 = v2 * sin(pose_2w(3));

pose_2w(1) = pose_2w(1) + vx2*dt;

pose_2w(2) = pose_2w(2) + vy2*dt;

pose_2w(3) = pose_2w(3) + omega2*dt;

Here, pose_2w(3) represents the robot ’s heading angle

This implementation ensures that the robot cannot move sideways (i.e., it obeys the non-holonomic constraint), and must rotate (omega2) to change its direction of travel —accurately modeling the real-world motion of a two-wheel differential-drive robot.

Although many robotic leg designs stack the hip and knee motors using a hollow-shaft configuration for compactness, hollow-shaft motors are significantly more expensive. In comparison, the HT8101 motors that are used currently in this project costs around $300 SGD, whereas a similar hollow-core motor would typically cost several times more. Furthermore, non-hollow motors can be positioned at both the front and rear of the chassis, allowing the mass to be evenly distributed. This improves overall balance and reduces the load concentration that typically occurs when hollow-shaft motors are stacked at a single joint.

To implement this configuration, a cascaded joint design is considered. This design is able to host two free rotating gear on the same axles, while each gear is transmitting the torque to the big leg and small leg respectively as shown in the video

<insert gif video >

Fabrication tolerance is critical, as excessive clearance can cause the bearings to rattle or produce noise. Although bearings are typically installed using a tight interference fit, a clearance fit is adopted here to make installation, disassembly, and troubleshooting easier.

The selected bearing is a bicycle-style headset bearing, chosen because it has a significantly thinner rim and is much lighter than standard ISO bearings. Its load-carrying capacity is more than sufficient —as shown in the simple calculation below —so size and weight become the primary selection criteria.

Due to limited manufacturer tolerance specifications for this bearing type, the cascaded bearing housing is designed with a 0.01 mm tolerance allowance to ensure proper fit without compromising stability.

5.3.2 Designs Details

As shown in the photo below, both the inner and outer rotating gear inner surfaces are supported by a pair of headset bearings each. Small chamfers are added to ensure the bearings seat properly and to prevent the fixed structure from interfering with their rotation. These chamfers ensure that the fixed part never contacts both the inner and outer bearing rings simultaneously, preserving smooth, unobstructed motion.

5.4 Hubless Wheel Design

5.4.1 Design Consideration

The hubless wheel design was chosen due to RoboMaster competition rules, which require rigid “armour plates ”to be mounted on all four sides of the robot. These plates must remain at a fixed height and stay horizontally aligned relative to the chassis during motion.

This constraint makes it difficult to use large conventional wheels in a bipedal wheel-leg configuration, as the armour plates would significantly obstruct the leg ’s movement. A hubless wheel avoids this interference, allowing the leg to articulate freely while still meeting the competition ’s armour-mounting requirements.

<insert picture if to mount armor plate on my robot >

With these constraints, a hubless wheel design was adopted. Although visually striking, hubless wheels are uncommon because they introduce additional bearing friction and offer less structural support compared to conventional hubs, making them mechanically less efficient and more difficult to implement. They also require a fully customised wheel-hub structure.

However, the design provides one advantage: it enables a much lower chassis profile, offering far greater flexibility in integrating the gimbal (turret) without exceeding the height limits imposed by the competition rules. And most importantly it is able to fit the armor plate rigidly in the centre of the wheel inner non-rotating frame.

5.4.5 Future Improvements

  1. The inner bearing of the large wheel requires redesign. The current turntable bearing is not suitable for radial loads —under high stress, it deforms easily and the balls can be forced into the raceway, causing the rotation to jam.
  2. Dynamic modelling of the gear train is also needed to determine the optimal gear ratio relative to the motor ’s torque characteristics.
  3. The mounting method between the inner frame and the leg should be revised, as the existing design includes several redundant components added solely to fit the wheel-leg structure, many of which are unnecessary.
  4. Finally, the overall mechanical structure must be strengthened to improve rigidity; excessive flex or compliance will negatively affect control performance and system stability.
